Medication Side Effect Detailed Reference for Pharmacy Review

Purpose

This companion file is for pharmacist and pharmacy intern preparation. It gives
more detailed side-effect information than the patient-facing counseling guide.
Do not read this file word-for-word to patients.

Use this file to:
- Choose the most relevant patient counseling points.
- Distinguish common side effects from less common but important warning signs.
- Identify which side effects need pharmacist review, monitoring, or escalation.
- Keep source tags next to the side-effect information.

For patient counseling, use medication_counseling_guide.txt.


Frequency Language

- Very common: about 10% or more.
- Common: about 1% to less than 10%.
- Less common: less than 1%, or not expected for most patients.
- Rare but serious: uncommon, but important because delayed recognition may
  cause harm.
- Varies: frequency depends heavily on exact medication, dose, route, duration,
  indication, kidney/liver function, age, comorbidities, and interacting drugs.

For exact medication-specific percentages, verify with DailyMed, the package
insert, Lexicomp, Micromedex, or the hospital-approved drug information
resource.


1. Antibiotics

Examples:
Azithromycin, cefazolin, cefepime, ceftriaxone, doxycycline, ertapenem,
piperacillin-tazobactam, vancomycin, linezolid, metronidazole, levofloxacin,
ciprofloxacin, daptomycin, gentamicin, tobramycin.

Common or expected side effects:
- Nausea, loose stools, mild diarrhea, abdominal discomfort, headache, and mild
  rash are common enough to mention for many antibiotics, but rates vary widely
  by antibiotic and regimen. [Source: DailyMed FDA label; CDC antibiotic side
  effects]
- Infusion-site discomfort, redness, or itching may occur with IV antibiotics;
  frequency varies by agent and infusion method. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Severe, watery, bloody, or persistent diarrhea may suggest C. diff or another
  clinically significant diarrhea syndrome. It is less common than mild loose
  stools but important to report early. [Source: CDC C. diff; DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Trouble breathing, swelling, hives, or severe rash may suggest serious
  allergy. This is rare but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- New confusion, seizure-like symptoms, severe dizziness, or unusual weakness
  can occur with selected antibiotics, especially with renal impairment or high
  exposure. This is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Daptomycin: muscle pain or weakness with CPK elevation is less common but
  important; eosinophilic pneumonia is rare but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Fluoroquinolones: tendon pain/rupture, neuropathy, CNS effects, QT-related
  symptoms, severe blood sugar changes, and aortic aneurysm/dissection risk in
  selected patients are less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Aminoglycosides: kidney injury, hearing changes, ringing in the ears, balance
  problems, and neuromuscular weakness are less common but important and often
  exposure-related. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Linezolid: thrombocytopenia, anemia, serotonin syndrome, lactic acidosis,
  neuropathy, and very low blood sugar are less common but important. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review allergies, renal function, culture data, duration, IV-to-oral plan,
  duplicate therapy, and drug levels when applicable.
- Escalate severe rash, breathing symptoms, severe diarrhea, mental status
  changes, seizure symptoms, or suspected toxicity.


2. Blood Thinners and Antiplatelets

Examples:
Heparin, enoxaparin, apixaban, rivaroxaban, warfarin, aspirin, clopidogrel,
ticagrelor.

Common or expected side effects:
- Bruising, minor bleeding, nosebleeds, or injection-site bruising can happen
  and are common enough to mention with anticoagulants or antiplatelets.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Mild injection-site pain or bruising can occur with injectable agents such as
  heparin or enoxaparin. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Vomiting blood or material that looks like coffee grounds is less common but
  serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Black or bloody stool is less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Blood in urine is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Severe headache, new weakness, confusion, fall, or head injury may indicate
  serious bleeding risk and should be escalated. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Bleeding that does not stop is less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia is uncommon but important because it can
  increase clotting risk despite low platelets. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Warfarin skin necrosis and purple toe syndrome are rare but serious. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review hemoglobin/hematocrit, platelets, renal function, INR for warfarin,
  procedure timing, neuraxial precautions, duplicate anticoagulation, and bleed
  history.
- Escalate any suspected major bleed, head injury, rapid hemoglobin drop, or HIT
  concern.


3. Diabetes Medications

Examples:
Insulin glargine, rapid-acting insulin such as insulin lispro, metformin,
empagliflozin, dapagliflozin.

Common or expected side effects:
- Insulin: hypoglycemia is common and clinically important; frequency varies by
  regimen, nutrition status, renal function, glucose targets, and illness.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label; MedlinePlus hypoglycemia]
- Insulin: injection-site reactions can occur; frequency varies by product and
  route. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Metformin: nausea, diarrhea, abdominal discomfort, and metallic taste are
  common, especially when starting or increasing dose. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- SGLT2 inhibitors: increased urination and genital mycotic infections are
  common enough to mention; UTI risk may occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Insulin: confusion, seizure, inability to swallow, or severe low blood sugar
  symptoms are serious and require prompt treatment. [Source: MedlinePlus
  hypoglycemia; DailyMed FDA label]
- Metformin: lactic acidosis is rare but serious, especially with renal
  dysfunction, hypoxia, severe illness, or excess alcohol use. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- SGLT2 inhibitors: euglycemic ketoacidosis, serious UTI/urosepsis, Fournier
  gangrene, and marked dehydration are rare or less common but serious. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review nutrition status, missed meals, tube feeds, steroid changes, renal
  function, perioperative status, and acute illness.
- Escalate severe hypoglycemia symptoms, DKA symptoms, dehydration, genital
  infection concerns, or lactic acidosis concern.


4. Heart Failure, Blood Pressure, and Diuretics

Examples:
Sacubitril-valsartan, lisinopril, losartan, carvedilol, metoprolol, furosemide,
bumetanide, torsemide, spironolactone, nitroglycerin.

Common or expected side effects:
- Dizziness, fatigue, lower blood pressure, and lightheadedness are common
  enough to mention, especially when starting or changing therapy. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- Loop diuretics: increased urination is expected; dizziness, dehydration,
  kidney function changes, and electrolyte changes can occur. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- Beta-blockers: fatigue, dizziness, lower heart rate, and lower blood pressure
  are common enough to mention. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Spironolactone: breast tenderness/gynecomastia and hyperkalemia risk may
  occur; frequency varies by population and dose. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Fainting, severe dizziness, chest pain, or trouble breathing are less common
  but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Very slow heart rate or feeling like the patient may pass out with beta-
  blockers is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Muscle weakness, severe cramps, or heart-rhythm symptoms may suggest
  electrolyte problems and are less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- ACE inhibitors: swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat is rare but
  serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Nitroglycerin or other vasodilators: severe hypotension or syncope is less
  common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review blood pressure, heart rate, renal function, potassium, magnesium,
  volume status, fall risk, and duplicate therapy.
- Escalate syncope, severe hypotension, angioedema symptoms, severe electrolyte
  symptoms, or acute kidney injury concern.


5. Breathing Medicines

Examples:
Albuterol, ipratropium-albuterol, tiotropium, fluticasone-salmeterol,
budesonide-formoterol.

Common or expected side effects:
- Albuterol: shakiness, nervousness, fast heartbeat, headache, and dizziness are
  common enough to mention. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Anticholinergic inhalers: dry mouth, throat irritation, constipation, or
  urinary symptoms may occur; frequency varies by product. [Source: DailyMed
  FDA label]
- Inhaled steroids: hoarseness, throat irritation, and oral thrush can occur;
  rinsing the mouth lowers thrush risk. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Chest pain, severe fast heartbeat, or feeling faint is less common but
  important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Paradoxical bronchospasm, meaning breathing gets worse right after treatment,
  is rare but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Pneumonia risk with inhaled steroid combinations is less common but important,
  especially in COPD. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Eye pain/blurred vision from nebulized anticholinergic exposure is less common
  but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review rescue vs maintenance inhaler use, duplicate inhalers, inhaler
  technique if discharge use is planned, and symptoms after treatment.
- Escalate paradoxical bronchospasm, severe tachycardia, chest pain, or
  pneumonia concern.


6. Transplant and Immune-System Medicines

Examples:
Tacrolimus, cyclosporine, mycophenolate, sirolimus, everolimus, belatacept,
antithymocyte globulin.

Common or expected side effects:
- Tacrolimus/cyclosporine: tremor, headache, blood pressure changes, kidney lab
  changes, potassium/magnesium changes, glucose changes, and infection risk are
  common to very common in some transplant populations. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Mycophenolate: diarrhea, nausea/vomiting, leukopenia, anemia, infection risk,
  and GI symptoms are common enough to mention. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Sirolimus/everolimus: edema, mouth sores, lipid changes, cytopenias, wound
  healing concerns, and infection risk may occur; frequency varies by regimen.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Antithymocyte globulin: fever, chills, infusion reactions, low white blood
  cells, low platelets, and infection risk may occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Fever, chills, sore throat, or other infection symptoms are important because
  infection risk is clinically significant. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Severe diarrhea, vomiting, or inability to keep medicines down is important
  because missed doses or altered levels can matter. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Severe tremor, confusion, seizure symptoms, or major alertness changes may
  suggest neurotoxicity or high levels and are less common but serious. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- Very low urine output, major swelling, or kidney problem concern is less
  common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Serious infusion reaction, anaphylaxis, serum sickness, severe cytopenias, or
  cardiopulmonary symptoms with antithymocyte globulin are less common but
  serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review transplant organ, regimen, drug levels, renal function, liver function,
  CBC, electrolytes, infection symptoms, and adherence/missed doses.
- Escalate suspected infection, severe cytopenias, high/low drug levels,
  nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, or infusion reaction.


7. Infection-Prevention Medicines for Transplant or Low Immunity

Examples:
Valganciclovir, ganciclovir, sulfamethoxazole-trimethoprim, atovaquone,
dapsone, pentamidine, fluconazole, voriconazole, posaconazole.

Common or expected side effects:
- Nausea, diarrhea, headache, and rash are common enough to mention for many of
  these medicines. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Blood count changes, kidney/liver lab changes, and electrolyte changes vary
  by medicine and are often detected by lab monitoring. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Voriconazole: visual changes are common enough to mention and may be
  transient. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Posaconazole and other azoles: GI symptoms, liver enzyme changes, and
  electrolyte abnormalities may occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Fever, chills, sore throat, unusual bruising, or signs of low blood counts are
  less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Severe rash or trouble breathing is rare but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Yellow skin/eyes, dark urine, or severe stomach pain may suggest liver injury
  and is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Dapsone: severe weakness, shortness of breath, blue/gray lips, dark urine,
  hemolysis, or methemoglobinemia symptoms are less common but serious. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- Pentamidine: breathing difficulty, blood pressure changes, kidney injury,
  pancreatitis symptoms, arrhythmia risk, blood sugar changes, or severe
  electrolyte abnormalities are less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review renal dosing, CBC, liver function, electrolytes, glucose, QT risk, and
  transplant medication levels when applicable.
- Escalate severe rash, cytopenia concern, liver injury concern, respiratory
  symptoms, hemolysis/methemoglobinemia concern, or QT/arrhythmia symptoms.


8. Pain Medicines: Non-Opioid

Examples:
Acetaminophen, ibuprofen, ketorolac, naproxen, lidocaine patches.

Common or expected side effects:
- Acetaminophen is usually well tolerated at safe doses; side effects are
  uncommon when the total daily dose is safe. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- NSAIDs can cause stomach irritation, nausea, dyspepsia, edema, higher blood
  pressure, kidney effects, and bleeding risk; frequency varies by dose,
  duration, and patient risk factors. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Lidocaine patches may cause local skin redness, irritation, or discomfort.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- NSAIDs: severe stomach pain, black/bloody stool, vomiting blood, unusual
  bleeding, kidney injury, fluid retention, or serious cardiovascular events are
  less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Acetaminophen: yellow skin/eyes, dark urine, severe nausea, or liver injury
  symptoms after excess exposure are rare but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Lidocaine: systemic toxicity is rare with correct patch use but serious if it
  occurs. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review total acetaminophen dose, liver risk, kidney function, bleeding risk,
  anticoagulants/antiplatelets, heart failure, and GI bleed history.
- Escalate suspected GI bleed, liver injury, kidney injury, or excess
  acetaminophen exposure.


9. Pain Medicines: Opioids

Examples:
Oxycodone, hydromorphone, morphine, fentanyl, hydrocodone-acetaminophen.

Common or expected side effects:
- Sleepiness, constipation, nausea, dizziness, itching, and fall risk are common
  enough to mention. [Source: DailyMed FDA label; FDA opioid safety
  communication]
- Constipation is common and often needs a bowel regimen when opioids continue.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Very slow or difficult breathing is less common but serious. [Source: FDA
  opioid safety communication]
- Extreme sleepiness, confusion, or being hard to wake is less common but
  serious. [Source: FDA opioid safety communication]
- Severe dizziness or a fall is less common but important. [Source: FDA opioid
  safety communication]
- Serotonin syndrome may occur with selected opioids and serotonergic medicines;
  this is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review pain control, sedation, respiratory status, bowel regimen, fall risk,
  renal function, opioid tolerance, duplicate sedatives, and naloxone if
  discharge opioids are planned.
- Escalate respiratory depression, oversedation, delirium, fall, or suspected
  overdose.


10. Steroids

Examples:
Prednisone, methylprednisolone, dexamethasone, hydrocortisone.

Common or expected side effects:
- Increased appetite, trouble sleeping, mood changes, fluid retention, and
  higher blood sugar are common enough to mention, especially with higher doses
  or longer courses. [Source: DailyMed FDA label; MedlinePlus prednisone]
- GI upset, blood pressure changes, and infection risk may occur; frequency
  varies by dose and duration. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Severe mood changes, confusion, psychosis, signs of infection, black/bloody
  stool, or very high blood sugar symptoms are less common but important.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label; MedlinePlus prednisone]
- Adrenal suppression is important with longer courses or abrupt discontinuation
  after prolonged use. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review glucose, blood pressure, infection symptoms, GI bleed risk, steroid
  dose/duration, taper plan, and discharge instructions.
- Escalate severe hyperglycemia, infection concern, GI bleed symptoms, severe
  psychiatric symptoms, or unclear taper.


11. Nausea Medicines

Examples:
Ondansetron, prochlorperazine, promethazine, metoclopramide.

Common or expected side effects:
- Sleepiness, dizziness, constipation, dry mouth, headache, or fatigue may occur;
  frequency varies by medicine. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Metoclopramide may cause drowsiness, fatigue, restlessness, or diarrhea.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Fainting, racing heartbeat, severe dizziness, or heart-rhythm symptoms are
  less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Metoclopramide or dopamine-blocking antiemetics: restlessness, tremor,
  stiffness, dystonia, tardive dyskinesia, or other unusual movements are less
  common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Serotonin syndrome is less common but important when serotonergic combinations
  are present. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review QT risk, sedation/fall risk, Parkinson disease, dopamine-blocking
  combinations, serotonergic medicines, and duration limits for metoclopramide.
- Escalate abnormal movements, severe sedation, QT/arrhythmia symptoms, or
  suspected serotonin syndrome.


12. Bowel Medicines

Examples:
Senna, polyethylene glycol, docusate, bisacodyl, lactulose.

Common or expected side effects:
- Cramping, gas, bloating, loose stools, and diarrhea are common enough to
  mention with bowel medicines. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Lactulose commonly causes gas, bloating, cramping, and diarrhea depending on
  dose and bowel-movement goal. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Severe diarrhea, severe stomach pain, dehydration symptoms, or no bowel
  movement when expected are less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Significant electrolyte abnormalities can occur with severe or prolonged
  diarrhea. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review bowel movement goals, stool frequency, dehydration risk, electrolytes
  when clinically relevant, opioid use, and lactulose indication.
- Escalate severe abdominal pain, persistent diarrhea, dehydration, or failure
  to meet bowel goals in hepatic encephalopathy.


13. Psychiatric, Sleep, and Anxiety Medicines

Examples:
Sertraline, escitalopram, quetiapine, haloperidol, trazodone, melatonin,
lorazepam.

Common or expected side effects:
- Sleepiness, dizziness, dry mouth, nausea, constipation, headache, or fall risk
  are common enough to mention for many of these medicines. [Source: DailyMed
  FDA label]
- SSRIs can cause GI upset, headache, sleep changes, sexual side effects, and
  may take weeks for full mood benefit. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Antipsychotics can cause sedation, orthostasis, dry mouth, constipation, and
  movement-related side effects. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Benzodiazepines can cause sedation, confusion, falls, dependence, and
  respiratory depression risk, especially with opioids or other sedatives.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label; FDA opioid/benzodiazepine safety communication]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Severe sleepiness, confusion, slowed breathing, or falls are less common but
  serious with sedating medicines. [Source: FDA opioid/benzodiazepine safety
  communication; DailyMed FDA label]
- New suicidal thoughts, severe mood changes, severe restlessness, stiffness,
  fever, abnormal movements, or neuroleptic malignant syndrome symptoms are less
  common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- QT-related symptoms are less common but important with selected agents.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review sedation, fall risk, QT risk, anticholinergic burden, serotonergic
  combinations, withdrawal/dependence risk, and indication.
- Escalate respiratory depression, severe confusion, abnormal movements, severe
  agitation, suicidal thoughts, or arrhythmia symptoms.


14. Seizure Medicines

Examples:
Levetiracetam, phenytoin, valproate, lacosamide, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine,
lamotrigine, topiramate, phenobarbital, gabapentin.

Common or expected side effects:
- Sleepiness, dizziness, trouble walking, nausea, headache, fatigue, or mood
  changes are common enough to mention for many seizure medicines. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- Levetiracetam: sleepiness/fatigue, dizziness, and behavior or mood changes may
  occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Phenytoin/fosphenytoin: dizziness, drowsiness, unsteady walking, slurred
  speech, abnormal eye movements, nausea, and gum changes may occur; neurologic
  symptoms can signal high levels. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Valproate: GI symptoms, sleepiness, dizziness, tremor, thrombocytopenia, and
  weight changes may occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Lacosamide: dizziness, sleepiness, headache, nausea, double vision, and
  unsteady walking may occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine: dizziness, drowsiness, nausea, vomiting,
  unsteady walking, low sodium, and rash may occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Lamotrigine: dizziness, headache, nausea, double vision, sleepiness,
  coordination problems, and rash may occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Topiramate: sleepiness, dizziness, tingling, appetite/weight change, slowed
  thinking, word-finding trouble, memory issues, and vision changes may occur.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Phenobarbital/gabapentin: sedation, dizziness, impaired coordination, and
  fall risk may occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Severe rash, blistering rash, mouth sores, or skin peeling with lamotrigine,
  carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, or other seizure medicines is rare but serious.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Severe confusion, unsteady walking, slurred speech, abnormal eye movements, or
  extreme sleepiness is less common but important and may suggest toxicity.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- New or worsening depression, suicidal thoughts, or major behavior changes are
  less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Valproate: liver injury, pancreatitis, hyperammonemia, severe thrombocytopenia,
  and serious birth-defect risk are less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed
  FDA label]
- Lacosamide: fainting, PR prolongation/heart rhythm changes, and serious
  allergic reactions are less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Topiramate: acute eye pain/vision loss, metabolic acidosis, kidney stones,
  overheating, and suicidal thoughts are less common but important. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- Gabapentin or phenobarbital with opioids, benzodiazepines, alcohol, or other
  sedatives: slowed breathing is less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label; FDA opioid/CNS depressant safety communication; FDA
  gabapentin/pregabalin safety communication]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review indication, renal/hepatic function, levels when applicable, albumin for
  phenytoin interpretation, sodium for carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine, CBC/LFTs for
  selected agents, pregnancy risk, sedation/falls, and discharge adherence plan.
- Escalate serious rash, neurologic toxicity, suicidal thoughts, respiratory
  depression, severe lab abnormalities, or uncontrolled seizures.


15. Electrolytes and Supplements

Examples:
Potassium, magnesium, phosphate, iron, vitamin D, thiamine, folic acid.

Common or expected side effects:
- Iron can cause constipation, dark stool, nausea, or stomach upset. These are
  common enough to mention. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Magnesium can cause diarrhea and stomach upset. These are common enough to
  mention. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Potassium can irritate the stomach, especially with oral tablets or liquid.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Severe stomach pain, severe diarrhea, vomiting, chest symptoms, severe
  weakness, or trouble swallowing tablets is less common but important. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- High or low potassium symptoms, severe weakness, palpitations, or arrhythmia
  symptoms are less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review renal function, electrolyte targets, route/formulation, GI tolerance,
  and timing with interacting oral medications if discharge use is planned.
- Escalate severe GI symptoms, trouble swallowing, severe weakness, palpitations,
  or major electrolyte abnormalities.


16. Alcohol Withdrawal or Substance-Use Medicines

Examples:
Lorazepam, diazepam, phenobarbital, thiamine, folic acid, multivitamin,
buprenorphine, methadone, naltrexone.

Common or expected side effects:
- Benzodiazepines and phenobarbital: sleepiness, dizziness, impaired
  coordination, confusion, and fall risk are common enough to mention. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- Buprenorphine/methadone: sleepiness, dizziness, constipation, sweating,
  nausea, and headache may occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Naltrexone: nausea, headache, dizziness, tiredness, and sleep changes may
  occur. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Less common but important warning signs:
- Very slow breathing, extreme sleepiness, confusion, fainting, or severe
  dizziness is less common but serious, especially with sedating combinations.
  [Source: FDA opioid/benzodiazepine safety communication; DailyMed FDA label]
- Methadone: chest palpitations, fainting, or QT-related symptoms are less
  common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Naltrexone: liver injury symptoms or precipitated opioid withdrawal are less
  common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Monitoring or escalation:
- Review sedation, respiratory status, withdrawal scoring/protocol, QT risk for
  methadone, liver function for naltrexone, opioid use history, and discharge
  follow-up plan.
- Escalate respiratory depression, oversedation, severe withdrawal, QT symptoms,
  liver injury concern, or unclear outpatient continuation plan.
