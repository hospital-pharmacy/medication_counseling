Medication Counseling Guide for Hospital Pharmacy

Purpose

This guide is for short, patient-friendly medication counseling in the hospital.
It is meant to help pharmacists and pharmacy interns explain new or changed
medications in plain language.

Use this guide to explain:
- What the medication is for
- How it helps
- The most useful side effects to know about
- What symptoms should be reported right away
- Simple safety reminders
- Whether the medication may continue after discharge, if known

This guide does not replace pharmacist judgment, the medication order, the care
team plan, the hospital drug information system, or FDA-approved labeling.

Pharmacy interns may share verified information, but they should not answer
clinical questions on their own. Questions that require judgment or a patient-
specific recommendation must be escalated to the supervising pharmacist.


Quick Bedside Script

Opening

"Hi, my name is _____. I am from the pharmacy team. I am here to talk with you
about new medications that were started in the hospital. Is now a good time?"

If family, visitors, or caregivers are present:

"I see you have someone with you. Is it okay if we talk about your medications
while they are here?"

Privacy reminder:
- Ask the patient's permission before discussing medications in front of family,
  visitors, or caregivers.
- If the patient wants privacy, pause until privacy is available.
- If a caregiver is the correct person to teach, follow hospital policy.


Simple Medication Counseling Template

Use this for each medication.

Medication name:
______________________________________________

Why you are receiving it:
"You are receiving this medication for ______________________________."

How it helps:
"This medication helps by ______________________________."

Common side effects:
"Some people may notice ______________________________. This is usually
manageable, but please tell your nurse or care team if it bothers you, gets
worse, or makes you feel unsafe."
[Source: ______________________________]

How often this happens:
"This is considered common / less common / rare but important. Exact chances
depend on the exact medication, dose, and your health situation."

Important warning sign:
"A less common but important warning sign is ______________________________.
If this happens, please tell your nurse or care team right away."
[Source: ______________________________]

Monitoring in the hospital:
"While you are here, the team may monitor ______________________________."

Discharge or home-medication safety point, only if relevant:
"If this medication continues after discharge, the pharmacist or care team may
review medicines, over-the-counter products, vitamins, or supplements to avoid
problems."

Discharge status, if known:
"Right now, this medication is expected to be continued / stopped / changed /
still being decided at discharge."

Patient questions:
"What questions do you have?"

Teach-back:
"Just so I know I explained it clearly, can you tell me what this medication is
for, or what symptom you should report?"


What Pharmacy Interns Should Do

Interns may:
- Introduce themselves as part of the pharmacy team
- Confirm whether now is a good time to talk
- Ask permission before speaking in front of family or visitors
- Share verified medication name, purpose, common side effects, warning signs,
  and simple safety points
- Use teach-back
- Tell the patient they will review questions with the pharmacist

Interns should escalate before answering questions about:
- Why a medication was started if the reason is unclear
- Whether a medication should be stopped, refused, restarted, or changed
- Dose changes, lab results, kidney or liver function, drug levels, or discharge
  plans
- New symptoms that may be side effects
- Allergic reaction, serious rash, swelling, trouble breathing, severe diarrhea,
  bleeding, chest pain, fainting, confusion, seizure symptoms, or severe
  dizziness
- Drug interactions, duplicate therapy, pregnancy/lactation, transplant
  medication levels, anticoagulation, insulin changes, opioid safety, or taper
  plans

Suggested intern wording:

"That is an important question. I want to make sure you get the correct answer,
so I am going to review it with the pharmacist."


Patient-friendly wording:

"Most people tolerate this medication. Some people may notice _____. Please tell
your nurse if it is bothering you or getting worse."

"A less common but more important symptom is _____. If that happens, tell your
nurse or care team right away."

Simple side-effect meaning:
- Common: happens often enough that it is useful to mention; usually manageable
- Less common: not expected for most people, but possible
- Rare but serious: not likely, but important to report right away
- Important: may need monitoring, treatment, or a dose change
- Urgent warning sign: tell the nurse or care team right away

Frequency wording for patient counseling:
- Very common: about 10% or more of patients
- Common: about 1% to less than 10% of patients
- Less common: less than 1% of patients, or not expected for most people
- Rare but serious: uncommon, but important because it may need urgent review
- Varies: the chance changes a lot by medication, dose, reason for use, kidney
  or liver function, length of therapy, and other patient factors

For broad medication groups, use frequency words instead of exact percentages
unless the exact medication and source are known. For exact numbers, verify with
DailyMed, the package insert, Lexicomp, Micromedex, or the hospital-approved
drug reference.


Medication Interaction Safety Checks

In the hospital, medication interactions are primarily the responsibility of the
pharmacist, provider, and care team. Patients usually do not control which
medications are administered while they are admitted.

Do not routinely counsel patients on inpatient drug-drug interactions.

Instead:
- The pharmacist or intern should screen for interactions before counseling.
- If an interaction concern is found, escalate it to the supervising pharmacist
  or care team.
- Discuss interactions with the patient only if they affect something the
  patient controls, such as home medications, over-the-counter products,
  vitamins, supplements, alcohol use, herbal products, or discharge medications.

Patient-friendly wording, only when relevant:

"While you are in the hospital, the care team checks your medications for
interactions. If this medication continues after discharge, pharmacy can review
what to avoid at home."

"Before taking new over-the-counter medicines, vitamins, supplements, or herbal
products after discharge, please check with your pharmacist or care team."


Common Medication Groups

Use these as quick patient-friendly prompts. Always confirm the patient-specific
reason before counseling.

Each medication group includes:
- Common side effects: symptoms that happen often enough to mention briefly.
- Less common or rare warning signs: symptoms that are not expected for most
  patients but should be reported right away because they may need prompt review.

These sections are a counseling overview, not a full adverse-effect list. For a
specific medication, verify exact frequency and warning details with DailyMed,
the package insert, Lexicomp, Micromedex, or the hospital-approved drug
reference.


1. Antibiotics

Examples:
Azithromycin, cefazolin, cefepime, ceftriaxone, doxycycline, ertapenem,
piperacillin-tazobactam, vancomycin, linezolid, metronidazole, levofloxacin,
ciprofloxacin.

Simple purpose:
"This antibiotic is being used to treat or prevent an infection. The plan may
change when the team gets more test or culture results."

Common side effects:
- Nausea, loose stools, mild diarrhea, headache, or rash may happen. These are
  generally common, but the chance varies by antibiotic and length of treatment.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label; CDC antibiotic side effects]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Severe, watery, bloody, or persistent diarrhea. This is less common but
  important. [Source: CDC C. diff]
- Trouble breathing, swelling, hives, or a severe rash. This is rare but
  serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- New confusion, seizure-like symptoms, severe dizziness, or unusual weakness
  with certain antibiotics. This is less common but important. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- New muscle pain or weakness with daptomycin. This is less common but
  important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Tendon pain, numbness/tingling, mood changes, or severe blood sugar changes
  with fluoroquinolones such as levofloxacin or ciprofloxacin. These are less
  common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen for interactions with vitamins/minerals, anticoagulants, seizure
medicines, heart-rhythm medicines, or transplant medicines. Discuss with the
patient only if it affects discharge or home-medication use.


2. Blood Thinners and Antiplatelets

Examples:
Heparin, enoxaparin, apixaban, rivaroxaban, warfarin, aspirin, clopidogrel,
ticagrelor.

Simple purpose:
"This medication lowers the chance of harmful blood clots or helps prevent
platelets from sticking together. Because it affects bleeding or clotting,
bleeding is the main safety point."

Common side effects:
- Bruising or minor bleeding can happen. This is common enough to mention with
  blood thinners.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Vomit that looks like blood or coffee grounds. This is less common but
  serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Black or bloody stool. This is less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Blood in urine. This is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Severe headache, new weakness, confusion, fall, or head injury. This is less
  common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Bleeding that will not stop. This is less common but serious. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen for duplicate anticoagulants/antiplatelets, NSAIDs, procedures, renal
function, and bleeding risk. Discuss OTC pain relievers only if relevant to
discharge or home use.


3. Diabetes Medications

Examples:
Insulin glargine, rapid-acting insulin such as insulin lispro, metformin,
empagliflozin, dapagliflozin.

Simple purpose:
"This medication helps manage blood sugar. In the hospital, blood sugar can
change because of illness, meals, tube feeds, steroids, surgery, or kidney
function."

Common side effects:
- Insulin can cause low blood sugar. This is common and clinically important.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label; MedlinePlus hypoglycemia]
- Metformin can cause stomach upset or diarrhea. This is common, especially
  when starting or increasing the dose. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- SGLT2 medicines such as empagliflozin or dapagliflozin can increase urination
  or genital/urinary infection risk. These are common enough to mention.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Shaking, sweating, confusion, weakness, fast heartbeat, or feeling like blood
  sugar is low. Low blood sugar is common with insulin and should be reported
  early. [Source: MedlinePlus hypoglycemia]
- Vomiting, severe weakness, fast breathing, or feeling very ill with diabetes
  medicines. This is less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Pain, redness, swelling, fever, or serious infection symptoms in the genital
  area with SGLT2 medicines. This is rare but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]

Patient-controlled safety point:
"Please tell staff if you miss a meal, cannot eat, vomit, or feel symptoms of
low blood sugar."


4. Heart Failure, Blood Pressure, and Diuretics

Examples:
Sacubitril-valsartan, lisinopril, losartan, carvedilol, metoprolol, furosemide,
bumetanide, torsemide, spironolactone, nitroglycerin.

Simple purpose:
"This medication helps with blood pressure, heart rate, fluid, or heart
protection."

Common side effects:
- Dizziness, tiredness, increased urination, or lower blood pressure can happen.
  These are common enough to mention, especially when doses are started or
  changed. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Fainting, severe dizziness, chest pain, or trouble breathing. This is less
  common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Very slow heart rate or feeling like you may pass out with beta-blockers.
  This is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Muscle weakness, severe cramps, or heart-rhythm symptoms that may suggest
  electrolyte changes. This is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat with ACE inhibitors such as
  lisinopril. This is rare but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen for potassium, kidney function, duplicate blood pressure therapy, and
medicines that may increase dizziness or low blood pressure. Routine patient
counseling should focus on symptoms to report.


5. Breathing Medicines

Examples:
Albuterol, ipratropium-albuterol, tiotropium, fluticasone-salmeterol,
budesonide-formoterol.

Simple purpose:
"This medication helps your breathing. Some inhalers or breathing treatments
work quickly, and others are maintenance medicines."

Common side effects:
- Shakiness or fast heartbeat can be common with rescue breathing medicines such
  as albuterol. Dry mouth, hoarseness, or throat irritation can also happen and
  frequency varies by inhaler. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Chest pain, severe fast heartbeat, or feeling faint. This is less common but
  important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Breathing that gets worse right after a treatment. This is rare but serious.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Fever, worsening cough, or symptoms of pneumonia with inhaled steroid
  combinations. This is less common but important, especially in COPD. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]

Medication history point:
"If you use inhalers at home, tell the team which ones you use."

Practical tip:
"If this inhaler contains a steroid and you continue it after discharge, rinse
your mouth after using it to lower the chance of thrush." [Source: DailyMed FDA
label]


6. Transplant and Immune-System Medicines

Examples:
Tacrolimus, cyclosporine, mycophenolate, sirolimus, everolimus, belatacept,
antithymocyte globulin.

Simple purpose:
"This medication lowers immune activity to help protect your transplant or treat
an immune-related problem."

Common side effects:
- Tremor, headache, diarrhea, nausea, infection risk, blood pressure changes,
  kidney lab changes, or blood count changes may happen. These are common to
  very common for some transplant medicines, but the exact chance varies by
  medicine and transplant regimen. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Fever, chills, sore throat, or other infection symptoms. Infection risk is
  common and can become serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Severe diarrhea, vomiting, or inability to keep medicines down. This is less
  common but important because missed doses can matter. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]
- Severe tremor, confusion, seizure symptoms, or major change in alertness.
  This is less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Very low urine output, major swelling, or concern for kidney problems. This is
  less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Transplant medicines have many clinically important interactions. Screen with
the pharmacist/transplant team. Discuss with the patient only for discharge,
home medicines, supplements, herbal products, or missed-dose instructions.


7. Infection-Prevention Medicines for Transplant or Low Immunity

Examples:
Valganciclovir, ganciclovir, sulfamethoxazole-trimethoprim, atovaquone,
dapsone, pentamidine, fluconazole, voriconazole, posaconazole.

Simple purpose:
"This medication helps prevent or treat infections while your immune system is
weaker."

Common side effects:
- Nausea, diarrhea, headache, and rash are common enough to mention for many of
  these medicines. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Blood count changes, kidney/liver lab changes, or electrolyte changes vary by
  medicine and are often found by monitoring labs. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Fever, chills, sore throat, unusual bruising, or signs of low blood counts.
  This is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Severe rash or trouble breathing. This is rare but serious. [Source: DailyMed
  FDA label]
- Yellow skin/eyes, dark urine, severe stomach pain, or concern for liver
  problems. This is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Severe weakness, shortness of breath, blue/gray lips, or dark urine with
  dapsone. This is less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen antifungals and antivirals for interactions with transplant medicines,
anticoagulants, statins, seizure medicines, and heart-rhythm medicines. Discuss
with the patient only if relevant to discharge or home use.


8. Pain Medicines: Non-Opioid

Examples:
Acetaminophen, ibuprofen, ketorolac, naproxen, lidocaine patches.

Simple purpose:
"This medication helps with pain or fever."

Common side effects:
- Acetaminophen is usually well tolerated at safe doses; side effects are
  uncommon when total daily dose is safe. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- NSAIDs such as ibuprofen, ketorolac, and naproxen can irritate the stomach and
  affect bleeding risk, blood pressure, or kidneys. These risks vary by dose,
  length of use, kidney function, bleeding risk, and other conditions. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Severe stomach pain, black/bloody stool, vomiting blood, or unusual bleeding
  with NSAIDs. This is less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Yellow skin/eyes, dark urine, or severe nausea after too much acetaminophen.
  This is rare but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen NSAIDs and acetaminophen for bleeding, kidney, liver, heart failure, and
duplicate-therapy risks. Discuss OTC pain medicine only if discharge or home use
is relevant.


9. Pain Medicines: Opioids

Examples:
Oxycodone, hydromorphone, morphine, fentanyl, hydrocodone-acetaminophen.

Simple purpose:
"This medication treats moderate to severe pain. In the hospital, nurses give
it based on the care team's orders and safety checks."

Common side effects:
- Sleepiness, constipation, nausea, and dizziness are common with opioids. Fall
  risk is clinically important, especially in the hospital. [Source: FDA opioid
  safety communication; DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Very slow or difficult breathing. This is less common but serious. [Source:
  FDA opioid safety communication]
- Extreme sleepiness, confusion, or hard to wake up. This is less common but
  serious. [Source: FDA opioid safety communication]
- Severe dizziness or a fall. This is less common but important. [Source: FDA
  opioid safety communication]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen for benzodiazepines, gabapentin/pregabalin, sleep medicines, alcohol use,
and other sedating medicines. For inpatient counseling, focus on reporting
excessive sleepiness or breathing problems.


10. Steroids

Examples:
Prednisone, methylprednisolone, dexamethasone, hydrocortisone.

Simple purpose:
"Steroids reduce inflammation or help with conditions such as breathing
problems, allergic reactions, autoimmune conditions, or adrenal problems."

Common side effects:
- Increased appetite, trouble sleeping, mood changes, fluid retention, and
  higher blood sugar are common enough to mention, especially with higher doses
  or longer courses. [Source: DailyMed FDA label; MedlinePlus prednisone]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Severe mood changes, confusion, signs of infection, black/bloody stool, or
  very high blood sugar symptoms. These are less common but important. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label; MedlinePlus prednisone]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen for diabetes therapy changes, NSAIDs, anticoagulants/antiplatelets, and
infection risk. Discuss discharge taper or home OTC use only if relevant.


11. Nausea Medicines

Examples:
Ondansetron, prochlorperazine, promethazine, metoclopramide.

Simple purpose:
"This medication helps prevent or treat nausea and vomiting."

Common side effects:
- Sleepiness, dizziness, constipation, or headache may happen. These are common
  enough to mention, but the chance varies by nausea medicine. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Fainting, racing heartbeat, severe dizziness, or heart-rhythm symptoms. This
  is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Restlessness, tremor, stiffness, or unusual movements with metoclopramide or
  similar medicines. This is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen for sedation, QT prolongation, dopamine-blocking combinations, and
serotonergic combinations. Patient counseling should focus on symptoms to report.


12. Bowel Medicines

Examples:
Senna, polyethylene glycol, docusate, bisacodyl, lactulose.

Simple purpose:
"This medication helps with constipation or helps meet bowel movement goals."

Common side effects:
- Cramping, gas, loose stools, or diarrhea are common enough to mention with
  bowel medicines. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Severe diarrhea, severe stomach pain, dehydration symptoms, or no bowel
  movement when the care team expected one. This is less common but important.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Patient-controlled safety point:
"Please tell staff about diarrhea, constipation, or missed bowel movements so
the plan can be adjusted."


13. Psychiatric, Sleep, and Anxiety Medicines

Examples:
Sertraline, escitalopram, quetiapine, haloperidol, trazodone, melatonin,
lorazepam.

Simple purpose:
"This medication may help with mood, anxiety, sleep, agitation, delirium, or
another mental health or neurologic symptom."

Common side effects:
- Sleepiness, dizziness, dry mouth, or nausea are common enough to mention for
  many of these medicines. Fall risk is clinically important in the hospital.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Severe sleepiness, confusion, slowed breathing, or falls with sedating
  medicines. This is less common but serious. [Source: FDA opioid/benzodiazepine
  safety communication; DailyMed FDA label]
- New suicidal thoughts, severe mood changes, severe restlessness, stiffness, or
  abnormal movements. These are less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA
  label]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen for opioids, benzodiazepines, sleep medicines, alcohol use, QT-risk
medicines, serotonergic combinations, and duplicate sedating therapy. Patient
counseling should focus on sedation, falls, breathing, and behavior changes.


14. Seizure Medicines

Examples:
Levetiracetam, phenytoin, valproate, lacosamide, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine,
lamotrigine, topiramate, phenobarbital, gabapentin.

Simple purpose:
"This medication helps prevent or treat seizures, or it may be used for another
condition such as nerve pain, mood, migraine prevention, or withdrawal. We
confirm the reason from your chart."

Common side effects:
- Sleepiness, dizziness, trouble walking, nausea, headache, or mood changes may
  happen depending on the medication. Many seizure medicines commonly cause
  sleepiness or dizziness, especially when starting or changing doses. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Severe rash, blistering rash, mouth sores, or skin peeling with medicines such
  as lamotrigine or carbamazepine. This is rare but serious. [Source: DailyMed
  FDA label]
- Severe confusion, unsteady walking, slurred speech, abnormal eye movements, or
  extreme sleepiness. This is less common but important and may suggest too much
  medication effect. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- New or worsening depression, suicidal thoughts, or major behavior changes.
  This is less common but important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Severe stomach pain, yellow skin/eyes, or unusual bleeding/bruising with
  valproate. This is less common but serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Slowed breathing when gabapentin or phenobarbital is combined with opioids,
  benzodiazepines, alcohol, or other sedating medicines. This is less common but
  serious. [Source: DailyMed FDA label; FDA opioid/CNS depressant safety
  communication; FDA gabapentin/pregabalin safety communication]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen seizure medicines for interactions, duplicate therapy, levels, renal or
hepatic dosing, and discharge medication changes. Discuss with the patient only
if relevant to discharge or home medication use.


15. Electrolytes and Supplements

Examples:
Potassium, magnesium, phosphate, iron, vitamin D, thiamine, folic acid.

Simple purpose:
"This replaces or supports something your body needs."

Common side effects:
- Iron can cause constipation or dark stool. This is common enough to mention.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]
- Magnesium can cause diarrhea. This is common enough to mention. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]
- Potassium can irritate the stomach. This is common enough to mention,
  especially with oral tablets or liquid. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Severe stomach pain, severe diarrhea, vomiting, chest symptoms, severe
  weakness, or trouble swallowing tablets. This is less common but important.
  [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen supplements for absorption issues with antibiotics, thyroid medicine, and
other oral therapies. Discuss timing with the patient only if the medication
will continue after discharge.


16. Alcohol Withdrawal or Substance-Use Medicines

Examples:
Lorazepam, diazepam, phenobarbital, thiamine, folic acid, multivitamin,
buprenorphine, methadone, naltrexone.

Simple purpose:
"This medication may help treat withdrawal, prevent vitamin problems, reduce
cravings, or support treatment for opioid use disorder."

Common side effects:
- Sleepiness, dizziness, nausea, constipation, or sweating may happen depending
  on the medication. Sleepiness and dizziness are common enough to mention with
  sedating withdrawal medicines and opioid-use-disorder medicines. [Source:
  DailyMed FDA label]

Tell the nurse or care team right away for:
- Very slow breathing, extreme sleepiness, confusion, fainting, or severe
  dizziness. This is less common but serious. [Source: FDA opioid/benzodiazepine
  safety communication; DailyMed FDA label]
- Chest palpitations or fainting with methadone. This is less common but
  important. [Source: DailyMed FDA label]

Medication history point:
"Please tell the team about anything you take at home or outside the hospital."

Pharmacy safety check, not routine patient counseling:
Screen for opioids, benzodiazepines, phenobarbital, alcohol use, sleep
medicines, QT-risk medicines, and other sedating medicines.


Documentation Prompts

Document per hospital workflow:
- Medication name
- Verified indication
- Main counseling points covered
- Important warning sign reviewed, if applicable
- Safety point reviewed, if applicable
- Interaction concern escalated or pharmacist-reviewed, if applicable
- Patient/caregiver understanding using teach-back
- Questions escalated to pharmacist or care team
